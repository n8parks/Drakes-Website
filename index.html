What Is Plugdata?

Plugdata is a free, visual Pure Data environment for creating and exporting audio patches as plugins (VST3, AU, LV2, CLAP).

Installing Plugdata

  1. Go to plugdata.org and download the installer for your OS (Windows, macOS, or Linux).
  2. Run the installer and launch Plugdata.

Step-by-Step Plugin Tutorial

Creating “Simple Delay and Reverb Plugin”

  1. Open Plugdata and make a new patch with your chosen name.
  2. Double click in the screen and type "adc~ 1 2". This is the analog-to-digital converter. It allows sound to come into the plugin.
  3. Next, go into the effects tab on the left. Drag the delay object into the screen. And then click the circle at the bottom left of the adc object and drag a connection to the top left circle of the delay object. Do the same for the right cirle of the adc and connect it to the same input as you did for the left.
  4. Back in the effects tab, drag and drop the Reverb object under the Delay object. Connect the bottom left output of delay to the top left input of Reverb.
  5. Double click under the Reverb and type "dac~". This is the digital-to-analog converter that sends the audio out of your patch and into your speakers. Drag the left output of Reverb to the left input of dac. Repeat the process for the right output and input.

Using the Delay Effect

Here’s what each Delay parameter does:

  • Time
    Sets how long the delay is—in other words, the gap between the original sound and its echo. A short Time (e.g. 50 ms) gives a slap-back effect; a long Time (e.g. 500 ms or more) creates distinct repeats.
  • Cutoff
    Controls the cutoff frequency of a built-in low-pass filter on the delayed signal. Turning Cutoff down rolls off high frequencies (making echoes sound darker), while turning it up passes more of the full spectrum through.
  • Resonance (Reason)
    Often paired with Cutoff, Resonance boosts frequencies around the cutoff point. Higher Resonance makes the filter peak—giving the delay a more pronounced “squelchy” or ringing character.
  • Feedback
    Determines how much of the delayed output is fed back into the delay line as input for the next repeat. Low Feedback (10–20 %) yields just a few echoes; high Feedback (70–100 %) makes repeats build up and can even self-oscillate.
  • Dry/Wet
    Balances the unprocessed (“dry”) signal with the delayed (“wet”) signal. At 0 % you hear only the original; at 100 % you hear only echoes. A middle setting blends both.
  • Clear
    When activated, immediately empties the delay buffer—cutting off any running echoes. Useful for resetting the effect cleanly between sections.

Using the Reverb Effect

Here’s what each Reverb parameter does:

  • Pre Delay
    Adds a short delay before the reverb tail begins. Small values (e.g. 20–50 ms) separate the initial sound from its reverberation; larger values create a sense of space or distance.
  • Size
    Controls the perceived room size. Smaller Size values simulate small rooms or chambers, giving a tighter, shorter decay. Larger values emulate halls or cathedrals with longer, more diffuse tails.
  • Damp
    Damping reduces high frequencies in the reverb tail. Turning Damp up makes the reverberation darker and less bright over time, mimicking soft or absorbent surfaces.
  • Cutoff
    Sets the cutoff frequency of a low-pass filter on the wet signal. Lower Cutoff rolls off more highs at the filter stage, while higher Cutoff passes more of the full spectrum into the reverb.
  • Dry/Wet
    Balances the original (“dry”) signal with the reverberated (“wet”) signal. 0 % means only the dry source; 100 % means only reverb. Middle settings blend both for natural ambience.
  • Clear
    When activated, immediately resets the reverb buffer and stops all ongoing reverberation. Useful for cutting off tails between sections or preventing buildup.
